# Retail Horizons Podcast — Episode 217

## "The 4,000-Item Problem: Inside Costco's Inventory Playbook"

**A Conversation with Ron Vachris, President & CEO, Costco Wholesale**

*Recorded March 2026 · Runtime: 38 minutes · Host: Dana Okafor*

---

**DANA OKAFOR:** Ron, thanks for making the time. I want to get right into it because inventory is one of those topics that most CEOs talk about in generalities, and I think Costco is genuinely different here. Before we dig into the specifics — how do you personally think about inventory? What's the mental model?

**RON VACHRIS:** Happy to be here, Dana. The mental model hasn't really changed in forty years, honestly. We sell a small number of things in very large quantities, and we try to sell them before we've paid for them. That's the whole business in one sentence. Everything downstream of that — the warehouse layout, the pallet displays, the buyer relationships, the Kirkland program — all of it flows from those two ideas.

**OKAFOR:** When you say "sell them before you've paid for them," you're talking about negative working capital, right? The suppliers effectively financing your inventory?

**VACHRIS:** That's the industry term for it, yes. Our inventory turns about twelve times a year. A typical supermarket might be at six or seven, and some big-box competitors are lower than that. So the cash comes in from the member well before the payable is due to the vendor. It's not magic — it's a function of carrying roughly four thousand active SKUs instead of forty thousand, moving them in pallet quantities, and being disciplined about what earns shelf space.

**OKAFOR:** Let's sit on the SKU count for a minute because I think people underestimate how radical that number is. Walmart Supercenter is north of a hundred thousand SKUs. You're at about four thousand. How does a buyer decide what makes the cut?

**VACHRIS:** It's the hardest job in our company and I say that without any exaggeration. Our buyers walk around every day knowing that if they want to bring in a new item, something has to come out. There's no version of this where we just add another shelf. So the question they're constantly asking is, does this product beat the one it's replacing on sell-through, on margin to the member, on basket impact? If it doesn't clearly win on those measures, we don't bring it in. Sentimentality is expensive in our model.

**OKAFOR:** Does that discipline ever hurt you? I can imagine there are products your members would love that just can't clear that bar.

**VACHRIS:** Of course. There are categories we don't serve well and members tell us about it. But if we tried to be everything to everyone, the whole economic engine stops working. The moment we're carrying fifteen thousand items instead of four thousand, our per-item volume drops, our leverage with suppliers drops, turns slow down, and suddenly we're a worse version of a conventional retailer. We'd rather be excellent at a narrow thing.

**OKAFOR:** I want to push on the storage question because I think there's a perception out there — and I've heard it from analysts — that the warehouse format means Costco is sitting on a huge amount of overhead storage cost. Walk me through why that's wrong, or if it's right.

**VACHRIS:** It's a reasonable question and the answer is that our stores *are* our storage. When you walk into a warehouse and you see pallets stacked fifteen, twenty feet high, that's not a design choice, that's inventory. We don't have a back room in the way a conventional grocer does. The selling floor and the storage facility are the same square footage. That eliminates a huge category of cost — we're not paying to move product from a stockroom to a shelf, we're not paying labor to face and merchandise every SKU, we're not paying for decorative fixtures. The product arrives on a pallet, the pallet goes on the floor, the member buys from it.

**OKAFOR:** And that's why the buildings look the way they look.

**VACHRIS:** That's exactly why. The concrete floors, the exposed ceilings, the utility lighting — every one of those is a deliberate cost decision. We don't spend money on things our members don't care about. A prettier store doesn't sell more rotisserie chickens.

**OKAFOR:** Let's talk about the pressure points, though, because I don't want this to sound like everything is frictionless. What's actually hard right now? What's keeping your merchandising team up at night?

**VACHRIS:** Tariffs, full stop. That has been the dominant operational variable for the last thirteen, fourteen months. Beginning in early 2025 with the IEEPA tariffs, then the Supreme Court decision, then the new global tariff framework that replaced them — our buyers have been navigating a policy environment that genuinely changes quarter to quarter. That's not the kind of thing you plan around with a spreadsheet.

**OKAFOR:** How have you actually responded?

**VACHRIS:** A few ways. First, we pulled forward some inventory buys last year where we saw exposure coming and it made sense to take delivery before the tariff hit. That's a short-term lever and it does cost us in carrying — it pushes against the turnover discipline I was describing earlier. But in that environment, taking the carrying hit was cheaper than taking the tariff hit. Second, we've been shifting production origins where we can. Some categories are moving toward domestic sourcing, some are moving between international partners. Third, and this is the longer-term play, we're leaning harder into Kirkland Signature because that's where we have the most control over the supply chain end to end.

**OKAFOR:** Did the pulled-forward inventory create any back-room problem, any storage crunch?

**VACHRIS:** It created pressure, I won't pretend it didn't. When you accelerate receipts by six or eight weeks across a set of categories, you feel it in the warehouses and in the depots. But our model is built for absorption more than most people realize. A pallet that arrives a month early just sits on the floor and gets sold. It's not like we had to lease additional space or stand up new distribution. The network flexed. What it did affect was our turnover rate temporarily, and we've been transparent with investors about that.

**OKAFOR:** What about backorders? When members can't find something they came for — is that a bigger problem now, or about the same?

**VACHRIS:** It's been a mixed picture. There were categories in holiday 2025 where we deliberately carried less — fewer artificial trees, less seasonal décor, fewer toys in some SKUs — because we couldn't source them at a price that would've delivered the value our members expect. That's a choice, not a failure. We'd rather not carry an item than carry it at a price we're not proud of. On the other side, our everyday staples have stayed in stock at rates we feel good about. The pharmacy operation is actually running above ninety-eight percent in-stock now, which is a number I couldn't have told you a few years ago.

**OKAFOR:** That's the AI reordering system?

**VACHRIS:** That's right. The pharmacy was our first meaningful deployment of predictive reordering, and the results have been strong enough that we're looking at how those principles extend to other categories. I want to be careful here because we're not going to become an algorithmically driven retailer in the way some of our competitors have positioned themselves. Our buyers are our advantage. But in categories where demand is reasonably predictable and the SKU set is stable, letting the system handle the reorder cadence frees those buyers to focus on the harder judgment calls.

**OKAFOR:** Let me ask about the treasure hunt, because this is where I think the inventory strategy gets genuinely interesting and a little counterintuitive. You *want* some items to sell out. You want the member to feel urgency.

**VACHRIS:** We want authentic urgency, which is a different thing. Manufactured scarcity would be a retailer saying "only five left" when there are five hundred in the back. We don't have a back, so when we say something's moving, it's moving. The rotating assortment in the center of the warehouse — the kayaks, the pianos, the seasonal items, the occasional luxury goods — those create the hunt. And the member relationship is built on the idea that if you see something you want, you buy it, because it may genuinely not be there next week.

**OKAFOR:** Does that make your inventory planning harder or easier?

**VACHRIS:** Easier, in a strange way. We don't owe the member continuity on those items. A traditional retailer has to maintain a planogram — if you carry a SKU, you carry it forever, and if you run out you've failed. Our members have consented to a different deal. They know the rotation is part of what they're buying. So we can take bigger swings on buys, and when a product is gone, it's gone, and that's okay.

**OKAFOR:** Last area I want to cover — the international and local sourcing shift. You mentioned Kirkland. Are you manufacturing more of your own product now?

**VACHRIS:** In specific categories, yes. The rotisserie chicken complex in Nebraska is the most often-cited example and for good reason — we did the math on freight, on supply stability, on quality control, and vertical integration was the right answer. We've done similar thinking on bottled water, where the product is heavy and the freight economics reward localizing production close to the warehouses that sell it. You'll see more of this, but selectively. We're not trying to become a manufacturer. We're trying to remove fragility from the parts of our supply chain where fragility costs us the most.

**OKAFOR:** If I had to summarize: inventory and storage costs are not a pain point for Costco — they're the thing you've optimized hardest around. The real operational story is tariffs and how you're adapting sourcing.

**VACHRIS:** That's fair. I'd add one thing to it, which is that the two are connected. The reason we can absorb tariff volatility as well as we have is that our inventory model gives us room to maneuver. If we were carrying six months of stock across forty thousand SKUs, we couldn't pivot the way we have. The discipline on turnover and SKU count is what buys us the flexibility. So when people ask about our inventory strategy as if it's a separate topic from our tariff response or our Kirkland strategy or our digital investment, I'd push back a little. It's all one system.

**OKAFOR:** Ron, this has been really useful. Thank you.

**VACHRIS:** Thanks for having me, Dana.

## Appendix: How Costco's Buying Is Actually Organized

A common question after reading a conversation like the one above is whether Costco has one person in charge of buying each product, or whether the process is automated. Based on what's publicly known, it's very much a human-driven system, not automated, with one notable exception.

Costco's buyers are famous inside the retail industry for being some of the most powerful individual decision-makers in the business. The reason is structural: when you only carry around 4,000 SKUs and each one moves enormous volume, every single buy is consequential. A buyer at a typical supermarket with 40,000+ SKUs is making decisions that each affect a small slice of sales. A Costco buyer's decision on, say, which olive oil to carry affects the entire olive oil category across 600+ U.S. warehouses — billions of dollars of volume concentrated in one person's judgment.

From what's publicly known about how it's organized: buyers are specialized by category (electronics, wine, bakery, apparel, and so on), not by individual product. One buyer might own "coffee" or "nuts" or "seasonal outdoor." There's a hierarchy — category buyers, senior buyers, and general merchandise managers above them — but the category buyer is the one who actually picks the item. They're given a lot of autonomy, and Costco is famously non-bureaucratic about merchandising decisions. If a buyer wants to bring in a new product, they don't run it through fifteen committees. Suppliers describe getting into Costco as notoriously hard because you're trying to convince one person, and that person has to believe the new item will beat whatever it displaces.

Where automation does play a role is primarily in the pharmacy, which is the main public example. Costco's AI-powered pharmacy reordering system now handles restocking autonomously and has pushed in-stock rates above 98%. But that's *reordering* — deciding how much of an already-approved SKU to send to which warehouse — not *buying*, which is the decision of what to carry in the first place. Those are very different jobs. The reordering and replenishment layer (how many pallets of Kirkland paper towels ship to a given warehouse next Tuesday) is increasingly algorithmic across retail, and Costco is moving that direction too. But the assortment decision — the "does this product earn a place" question — is still a human call, and Costco seems committed to keeping it that way. Vachris has been pretty explicit in earnings calls that the buyers are the competitive advantage.

Short answer: one buyer per category, humans decide what to carry, algorithms increasingly decide how much to ship where.
