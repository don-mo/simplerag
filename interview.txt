Managing Conflict
Personal conflicts:
Personal conflicts may arise between team members due to differences in personality, values, or communication style.
Encourage open communication:
Encourage team members to communicate openly and honestly with each other, and create an environment where it is safe for team members to express their thoughts and feelings.
Facilitate a resolution:

Encourage mediation:
If necessary, consider bringing in a mediator to help facilitate resolution. This could be an external mediator, or someone within the company who is trained in conflict resolution.
Address underlying issues:
Look for underlying issues that may be contributing to the conflict, and work to address those issues to prevent future conflicts.

It is generally not a good idea to hide problems from your startup team. In fact, being open and transparent about problems can be an important part of building trust and fostering a culture of teamwork and collaboration.

Lack of trust: Hiding problems can lead to a lack of trust within the team, as team members may feel that they are being kept in the dark.
Inefficient problem-solving: By hiding problems, you may be missing out on the valuable insights and perspectives of your team members, which can hinder your ability to effectively solve problems.
Reduced productivity: Hiding problems can lead to a lack of transparency and accountability, which can in turn lead to reduced productivity and efficiency.
Negative impact on morale: Hiding problems can create a sense of uncertainty and lack of direction, which can have a negative impact on team morale.

In general, it is best to be open and transparent about problems, and to involve your team in finding solutions. This can help to foster a culture of teamwork and collaboration, and will ultimately be more beneficial for Eat Together.

Culture 🎉
1.	Create open environments for people to independently work as well as to do things together.
○	Use jam sessions not only as work time, but also as time to collaborate on and discuss important things with people.
■	Be mindful of people’s work style preferences (e.g., some may prefer async work or work alone, and that’s ok!).

2.	Empower and encourage your team members.
○	Give shoutouts to people who do a good job or go above and beyond!
○	Use encouraging words to those who do well and those who might be struggling with work.
3.	Help your team members “own” what they are working on.
○	Although it’s tempting to do everything yourself or “carry” the project, this doesn’t create a good collaborative environment for everyone.
■	A proverb that I like: “If you want to go fast, go alone. If you want to go far, go together.”
■	This doesn’t mean that you can’t provide help, though; having async/synchronous check-ins and asking questions helps to see what kind of assistance they need!
○	The more that they feel in charge of their own projects/tasks, the more they feel motivated to work, and the less work you have to do to “micro-manage” them.
4.	Understand people’s needs, and serve them.
○	Understand why people want to work for Eat Together, and do your best to create that kind of environment for them.
■	Example 1: passionate about the mission. Give them opportunities to interact and eat with students/users!
■	Example 2: professional growth. Provide projects with great learning opportunities and challenges to overcome, mentoring them along the way. Help with resume review and interview prep for other opportunities they want to pursue!
○	Think of ways to go above and beyond (personalization)!
○	Relationships are not about “fair trades”, but about being there for someone when they need it.
5.	Do things with good intentions, and be genuine about them.
○	This especially applies to tough things like giving feedback. Frame it as a way to help the person.
6.	Eat Together. No, I mean literally!
○	Given the mission to bring students over food, an easy and great way is to start with your team!
○	A quick “hey, anyone want to grab food after this meeting?” can go a long way to getting to bond with your fellow teammates. Obviously, don’t force yourself/others into this if you/they are not willing.

People and Conflicts 🤔
This is one of the crucial parts of being a director—your people skills. Your job isn’t necessarily to do the tasks that you can assign to others, but rather to serve their needs and ensure that they have the capability, tools, and support to accomplish what they need to do. Sometimes, though, conflicts will undoubtedly arise, and you may feel pressured as a leader to take ownership of them and try to resolve them as fast as possible. It’s good to take ownership, and it’s normal to feel overwhelmed sometimes, but remember to be patient. Conflict resolution isn’t about quick fixes, but it’s about building an open space to hear people’s voices so that you and the team can find the root cause and take the right next steps. And remember that you’re not alone in this—lean on team members, mentors, trusted friends—and use this discomfort as a learning opportunity to come out stronger.

Here are 2 common scenarios that you may have to deal with, as well as my advice on how to navigate them:

Team Member Continued Absence/Lack of Work
First of all, NEVER jump to conclusions. Just because someone is slacking doesn’t mean that they’re doing it intentionally. Reasons could include: unexpectedly busy schedule, emergency situations, personal/family matters, …
You want to make sure that you understand the root cause. Ask them a quick message, like “Hey, I noticed that you haven’t been as active as before in Eat Together; just wanted to check in and see how you’re doing?”
If they aren’t responsive:
●	Follow up after 2-3 days.
●	Use a different way to contact them (e.g. email, phone number).
Hopefully, you get a response from them by this point! Once you’ve established communication, try to chat 1-1 synchronously (in-person or online) to better understand their situation. Be an active listener, be empathetic and forgiving, but also try to gauge their motivation/interest in continuing with Eat Together.
If they don’t seem motivated to work for Eat Together, it may be best to let them go. Otherwise, great! Try to come up with alternate ways for them to continue staying involved. Some examples of things to consider:
●	Have a weekly sync time (or office hours, however you want to call it) specifically for them, especially if they can’t make the usual weekly meetings.
●	Use a different method of communication if Discord (or insert other communication tool here) isn’t their thing.
●	Lower the scope of their task/project. See if others can help!

Disagreements in Group Discussions
Given the diversity of thought, this is bound to happen. A straightforward solution would be to approach the conflict democratically (i.e., “most votes”). This works for small decisions like time scheduling, where to eat, etc., but a lot of cases are more complicated than a simple vote …
First, ensure that everyone has the same main objective(s) in mind. If people don’t get why they’re making the decision that they’re making, or if it’s off-tangent with the main goal(s), then it’s hard to justify it. For instance, at an Amazon internship, I once disagreed with my manager about making changes to other internal teams’ codebases regarding a change to a dependency I made. I wanted to make the changes for customer satisfaction, but my manager wanted to skip this due to the tight timeline of the internship; in other words, I prioritized quality while my manager prioritized speed. Eventually, we both agreed that quality would be a more important main objective, hence agreeing to the same decision of making the code changes for other teams.
Next, create a comfortable, open environment for everyone to share their opinions. Lead the discussion—try to reach a common ground—encourage everyone to listen attentively to each other, and promote good team conduct. Ask clarifying questions whenever something isn’t clear, and avoid bias/emotion when judging thoughts.
Once everyone has finished sharing their thoughts, encourage them to come up with a solution/solutions together, and carefully consider the pros and cons of each. If needed, take more time outside the discussion to reflect and come up with a final decision, getting more input from others if needed. Once you’ve made a decision, communicate it clearly to everyone, and if it compromises things some people have said, make sure that they’re okay with it.

 
Getting Stuff Done 💪
1.	Ensure that people understand the “why” of what they’re doing.
a.	Purpose gives motivation for doing things.
b.	There should be meaning to everything, even if it’s small.
c.	Sometimes, it’s also about the journey, not the result. If you learned a lot from doing something, then that’s meaningful!
2.	Break down a big task into smaller tasks, until those tasks are clear in stating what the person should specifically do.
a.	E.g., restaurant outreach -> reach out to Cafe on the Ave -> go eat at the restaurant and talk to manager about proposal
b.	It’s alright if the final deadline isn’t clear, but make it clearer as you get closer to the finish line.
c.	Think about what the person needs to be able to accomplish the task(s) (skills, tools, etc.), and help them with that if needed.
3.	Make sure that the deadlines for small tasks are clear, and have progressive check-ins for these to see if extensions or lowering the scope may be required or not.
a.	We use ClickUp to track these deadlines.
b.	Try to make these tasks due in no later than 2 weeks, so that people don’t feel overwhelmed or lose focus. Industry loves 2-week sprints for a reason.
i.	Exception: anything that has a rigid quarter schedule, like a marketing schedule.
c.	Be mindful of busy times, like midterms and finals.
4.	Give appropriate motivation and recognition!
a.	Words of encouragement and praise can go a long way. It may sound annoying/unnatural at first, but over time, you’ll see the value in this, especially in a school environment that kinda lacks these interactions.
b.	Going the extra mile: surprise members with food or small gifts!
